What makes Belgium tick?

History

1830 - Belgium becomes the independent state, separating from the Netherlands. From this point into the late 19th century, the country faced a three-way language struggle between Dutch, French and German languages.

In 1963, Belgium was divided into three language areas:

  • The Dutch-speaking region: Flanders (the provinces of Antwerp, Limburg, Flemish Brabant, East and West Flanders)
  • The French-speaking region: Wallonia (the provinces of Hainaut, Liege, Namur and Walloon Brabant)
  • The German-speaking region (the nine municipalities of the East Cantons)
  • The bilingual Brussels-Capital Region (the 19 Brussels municipalities) with six Dutch-speaking suburban municipalities with administrative language facilities for the French-speaking inhabitants.

Each of the different communities are responsible for different aspects of social, cultural and educational policies across the entirety of Belgium. Flanders, the dutch region of Belgium bordering the Netherlands, being the seat of the Ministry of Education

Education System

In it's current form, the education is compulsory in some form from the age of five until eighteen. Pre-school is avaialble to children from the age of two to six but is not mandatory.

Students are granted a certificate at the completion of each academic stage e.g completion of Primary/Secondary education.

Secondary education comprises of three stages, each stage has two grades within.

At stage one, students are given a broad, general education and does not focus on a specific subject area but lays groundwork for development in a specialsied subject area.

From stage two, different options are given to students:

  • General secondary education (gse), which focuses on broad general education. It does not prepare pupils for a specific profession, but rather lays a firm foundation for higher education.
  • In technical secondary education (tse) attention goes in particular to general and technical-theoretical subjects. After tse a youngster may practice a profession or transfer to higher education. This type of education also contains practical training.
  • Secondary education in the arts combines a broad general education with an active practice of art. After secondary education in the arts a youngster may practice a profession or transfer to higher education.
  • Vocational secondary education (vse) is a practically-oriented type of education in which the youngster receives general education but where the focus primarily lies on learning a specific profession.

Upon completion of stage three, the student is presented with a certificate of upper secondary education, granting them access to higher education.

Similarly to the United Kingdom, Belgium operates an alterantive "Learning and Working" scheme for young people aged fifteen to eighteen in place of stage three of their education.
